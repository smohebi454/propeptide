Retatrutide Clinical Trials: Phase 2 Results & TRIUMPH Phase 3 Program (2026)

March 20, 2026 — Dr. Lara Vestri, PharmD

Quick Answer: The Phase 2 retatrutide trial (published NEJM, June 2023) enrolled 338 adults with obesity. At the highest dose (12mg), participants lost 24.2% of body weight at 48 weeks — the largest reduction recorded for any anti-obesity compound in a controlled trial. Eli Lilly’s Phase 3 TRIUMPH program (4 trials, 4,000+ participants) began in 2023 with topline results from TRIUMPH-4 reported in December 2025.

Phase 2 Trial Design

The pivotal Phase 2 trial for retatrutide (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier NCT04881706) was published in The New England Journal of Medicine in June 2023, marking the first large-scale evaluation of a triple-agonist compound for obesity treatment. The study enrolled 338 adults with a body mass index (BMI) of 30 or greater, or a BMI of 27 or greater with at least one weight-related comorbidity. Participants were recruited across multiple clinical sites in the United States.

The trial employed a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled design — the gold standard for evaluating pharmaceutical efficacy. Participants were assigned to one of seven groups: six active dose cohorts (1mg, 4mg with slow titration, 4mg with rapid titration, 8mg with slow titration, 8mg with rapid titration, and 12mg) plus a placebo arm. Each cohort followed a defined titration schedule to manage tolerability, with dose escalation occurring over the first 12 to 20 weeks depending on the assigned group.

The primary endpoint was percentage change in body weight from baseline at 48 weeks. Secondary endpoints included the proportion of participants achieving clinically meaningful weight loss thresholds of 10% and 15%, along with changes in cardiometabolic risk markers including HbA1c, fasting glucose, lipid panels, and blood pressure. The 48-week treatment duration provided sufficient data to evaluate both the magnitude and durability of the dose-response relationship across all cohorts.

Dose-Response Results

The Phase 2 data revealed a clear and consistent dose-dependent relationship across all cohorts, with every active dose group significantly outperforming placebo. The results demonstrated that higher doses produced progressively greater weight loss, while the titration speed (slow vs. rapid) had minimal impact on final outcomes at matched doses. Below is the complete dose-response data from the 48-week trial.

Dose Titration Weight Loss (%) ≥10% Threshold ≥15% Threshold
Placebo — -2.1% 9% 2%
1 mg Standard -8.7% 33% 11%
4 mg Slow -17.1% 75% 54%
4 mg Rapid -16.8% 73% 50%
8 mg Slow -22.1% 88% 75%
8 mg Rapid -22.8% 90% 77%
12 mg Slow -24.2% 93% 83%

Source: Jastreboff AM et al. Triple–Hormone-Receptor Agonist Retatrutide for Obesity — A Phase 2 Trial. N Engl J Med. 2023;389:514-526.

The 12mg cohort stands out not only for the -24.2% mean weight reduction but for the consistency of response: 93% of participants achieved at least 10% weight loss, and 83% exceeded the 15% threshold. These response rates far surpass those reported in comparable Phase 2 trials of semaglutide and tirzepatide at their respective highest doses. The fact that even the lowest active dose (1mg) produced a clinically meaningful -8.7% reduction underscores the potency of the triple-agonist mechanism.

Safety & Tolerability

The safety profile of retatrutide in the Phase 2 trial was consistent with expectations for the GLP-1 receptor agonist drug class, with gastrointestinal (GI) adverse events being the most commonly reported side effects. Nausea, diarrhoea, vomiting, and constipation were the predominant complaints, and their incidence followed a dose-dependent pattern. At the 1mg dose, GI events were mild and infrequent. At 4mg and 8mg, approximately 40–50% of participants reported at least one GI event, while the 12mg cohort saw rates approaching 60%.

Critically, the titration approach had a meaningful impact on tolerability. Groups that underwent slower dose escalation reported lower peak severity of nausea and vomiting during the titration phase compared to rapid-titration groups at the same target dose. Most GI symptoms were transient, resolving within the first 4–8 weeks of reaching the maintenance dose as the body adapted to triple-receptor activation.

Discontinuation rates due to adverse events ranged from approximately 6% to 8% across all dose groups — comparable to dropout rates observed in Phase 2 and Phase 3 studies of semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound). No unexpected safety signals emerged during the trial. Cardiovascular monitoring showed a modest increase in resting heart rate of +2 to +4 beats per minute at higher doses, a finding consistent with other incretin-based therapies. Hepatic, renal, and pancreatic safety markers remained within normal ranges. Side effect data by dose cohort is reviewed in detail in Remy Peptides' adverse event analysis.

Body Composition Substudy

One of the most significant differentiators for retatrutide emerged from a prespecified body composition substudy published in The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology. This analysis used dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) scans — the gold standard for distinguishing between fat mass and lean mass changes — to evaluate what type of tissue participants were actually losing. The results fundamentally changed the narrative around retatrutide.

DXA data showed that retatrutide produced preferential fat loss. The ratio of fat mass lost relative to total weight lost was significantly higher than what has been observed in comparable studies of semaglutide and tirzepatide. In practical terms, participants on retatrutide retained a greater proportion of their lean body mass (muscle, bone, organ tissue) while shedding fat at an accelerated rate. This finding is attributed directly to the glucagon receptor component of the triple-agonist mechanism, which drives hepatic fat oxidation and increases resting energy expenditure independently of caloric restriction.

The body composition advantage is clinically meaningful because lean mass preservation is strongly correlated with long-term metabolic health, functional capacity, and reduced risk of weight regain. Single-agonist GLP-1 therapies have faced persistent criticism for producing weight loss where 20–40% of the total comes from lean tissue. Retatrutide’s DXA data suggests a fundamentally better body composition outcome — not just more weight loss, but a higher quality of weight loss. TRIUMPH Phase 3 Program

Building on the Phase 2 results, Eli Lilly launched the TRIUMPH clinical trial program in late 2023 — one of the most ambitious Phase 3 programs in obesity drug development. TRIUMPH comprises four distinct registration trials designed to evaluate retatrutide across the full spectrum of patient populations and treatment scenarios required for regulatory approval.

  • TRIUMPH-1: Evaluates retatrutide in adults with obesity (BMI ≥30) or overweight (BMI ≥27) with at least one weight-related comorbidity, but without type 2 diabetes. This is the primary efficacy trial for the general obesity indication.
  • TRIUMPH-2: Focuses specifically on adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes, assessing both weight loss and glycaemic control endpoints including HbA1c reduction.
  • TRIUMPH-3: A long-term weight maintenance study evaluating whether weight lost during initial treatment is sustained over an extended period, addressing the critical question of durability that regulators require.
  • TRIUMPH-4: Topline results were reported in December 2025, confirming statistically significant and clinically meaningful weight reduction consistent with Phase 2 findings. Full detailed data is anticipated for publication and presentation at major medical conferences in 2026.

The combined TRIUMPH program enrolls more than 4,000 participants across multiple countries, providing the statistical power and geographic diversity required for global regulatory submissions. The TRIUMPH trial program is tracked in real-time by Remy Peptides' TRIUMPH tracker. Based on current timelines, Eli Lilly is expected to file for regulatory approval with the FDA and EMA in the 2026–2027 window. What This Means for Research

The clinical significance of retatrutide extends well beyond the headline weight loss figures. The Phase 2 data established several important precedents. First, a 24.2% mean weight reduction at 48 weeks exceeds the efficacy benchmarks set by every currently approved anti-obesity medication, including tirzepatide (which achieved approximately 22.5% in its pivotal SURMOUNT-1 trial at 72 weeks). The fact that retatrutide reached higher absolute weight loss in a shorter treatment window underscores the potency of triple-receptor engagement.

Second, the body composition data introduces a qualitative dimension that single-agonist and dual-agonist therapies have not matched. Preferential fat loss with lean mass preservation addresses one of the most significant clinical concerns in metabolic medicine: the metabolic consequences of losing muscle during pharmacological weight management.

Third, the therapeutic implications extend beyond obesity into adjacent metabolic conditions. Early data and ongoing investigations suggest potential efficacy in metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH, formerly NASH), non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and cardiovascular risk reduction. The glucagon receptor component, which drives hepatic fat oxidation, is of particular interest for liver-related conditions where fat accumulation in hepatocytes is the primary driver of disease progression. Frequently Asked Questions

What were the Phase 2 retatrutide results?

The Phase 2 trial demonstrated 24.2% body weight loss at the highest dose (12mg) over 48 weeks, with a clear dose-dependent response observed across all cohorts. This was the largest weight reduction recorded for any anti-obesity compound in a controlled clinical trial.

How many people were in the retatrutide trial?

The Phase 2 trial enrolled 338 adults with a BMI of 30 or higher, who were randomized across 6 dose groups (1mg, 4mg slow, 4mg rapid, 8mg slow, 8mg rapid, and 12mg) plus a placebo control arm.

What is the TRIUMPH program?

TRIUMPH is Eli Lilly’s Phase 3 clinical trial program for retatrutide, consisting of 4 large-scale trials (TRIUMPH 1 through 4) enrolling more than 4,000 participants across multiple countries to evaluate efficacy and safety in different patient populations.

When will Phase 3 results be available?

TRIUMPH-4 topline results were reported in December 2025, confirming significant weight reduction. Full data from all 4 TRIUMPH trials is expected throughout 2026, with potential regulatory submission in 2026–2027.

What journal published the retatrutide data?

The Phase 2 results were published in The New England Journal of Medicine (NEJM) in June 2023. The full citation is: Jastreboff AM et al. Triple–Hormone-Receptor Agonist Retatrutide for Obesity — A Phase 2 Trial. N Engl J Med. 2023;389:514-526.

Did anyone drop out of the retatrutide trial?

Discontinuation due to adverse events was approximately 6–8% across dose groups, which is comparable to dropout rates seen with other GLP-1 receptor agonist class compounds in similar clinical trials.
