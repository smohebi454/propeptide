Peptide Purity Testing: How HPLC & Mass Spectrometry Verify Research Compounds (2026)

March 20, 2026 — Dr. Lara Vestri, PharmD

Quick Answer: HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) measures peptide purity by separating compounds and quantifying the target molecule as a percentage of total content. Mass spectrometry (MS) confirms molecular identity by measuring exact molecular weight. Together, they form the gold standard for peptide verification. ProPeptide’s current batch (RETP002) tested at 99.262% purity via HPLC by Janoshik Analytical.

Why Purity Testing Matters

Purity is not a marketing claim — it is the single most important variable in peptide research. When a compound is labelled as a specific peptide, the only way to know what is actually in the vial is analytical testing. Without it, every downstream result is unreliable.

Research integrity depends on knowing the exact composition of the compound being studied. A peptide sample advertised at 95% purity contains up to 5% unknown material — that could be truncated sequences, synthesis byproducts, residual solvents, or degradation products. At research-relevant doses, those contaminants can introduce confounding variables, alter receptor binding kinetics, or produce unexpected biological responses that have nothing to do with the peptide itself.

The difference between 95% and 99%+ purity is not marginal. It represents a fivefold reduction in contaminant load. For dose-dependent studies where microgram-level accuracy matters, that gap is the difference between reproducible results and noise. Contaminants like trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) from synthesis, acetonitrile residues, or truncated peptide fragments can all be detected and quantified through proper analytical testing. Without that data, researchers are working blind.

Purity testing also serves as a check on the entire supply chain. It verifies that the manufacturer’s synthesis was successful, that shipping and storage did not degrade the compound, and that what was ordered is what was received.

HPLC: Measuring Purity

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is the primary method for measuring peptide purity. It works by physically separating the components of a sample and then quantifying each one individually. The result is a precise percentage that tells you how much of your sample is actually the target peptide versus everything else.

The process begins when the peptide sample is dissolved and injected into a column packed with a stationary phase — typically C18 silica beads. A mobile phase (a solvent mixture, usually water and acetonitrile with a small amount of TFA) is pumped through the column at high pressure. Different molecules interact with the stationary phase differently: some pass through quickly, others are retained longer. This differential retention separates the target peptide from impurities.

As each component exits the column, it passes through a UV detector (typically set at 214 nm or 220 nm, wavelengths where peptide bonds absorb strongly). The detector records a chromatogram — a graph of signal intensity over time. Each compound appears as a distinct peak. The target peptide produces the dominant peak, and any impurities show as smaller peaks at different retention times.

Purity is calculated by peak area integration: the area under the main peak divided by the total area of all peaks, expressed as a percentage. When we report “99.262% purity,” it means that 99.262% of the UV-absorbing material in the sample is the target peptide. The remaining 0.738% consists of trace impurities — synthesis byproducts, truncated sequences, or minor degradation products, each individually below the threshold of concern.

Mass Spectrometry: Confirming Identity

While HPLC tells you how pure a sample is, mass spectrometry (MS) tells you what the molecule actually is. These are fundamentally different questions. A sample could be 99.9% pure by HPLC but contain the wrong peptide entirely. Mass spectrometry eliminates that possibility by confirming molecular identity at the atomic level.

Mass spectrometry works by ionising the peptide molecules and then measuring their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). The instrument generates a mass spectrum — a fingerprint of molecular weight that is unique to each peptide sequence. If the measured molecular weight matches the theoretical molecular weight calculated from the amino acid sequence, identity is confirmed.

This is particularly important for catching substitution errors (where one amino acid is swapped for another during synthesis) and truncation errors (where the peptide chain is shorter than intended). Both would produce a different molecular weight that MS detects immediately, even if HPLC purity appeared acceptable.

The two most common MS methods for peptides are electrospray ionisation (ESI-MS) and matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionisation (MALDI). ESI-MS is typically coupled directly with HPLC (LC-MS) for simultaneous purity and identity analysis. MALDI is faster for single-sample identification and works well for larger peptides. Both deliver molecular weight accuracy sufficient to distinguish a single amino acid substitution.

Reading a Certificate of Analysis (COA)

A certificate of analysis is the document that puts all of this data into a standardised format. It is issued by the testing laboratory for a specific batch and includes every test performed, the method used, the result, and the specification it was measured against. Understanding how to read a COA is essential for evaluating any peptide supplier’s quality claims.

Here is what a real COA looks like, using our current batch (RETP002) tested by Janoshik Analytical as an example:

Test Method Result Specification
Purity HPLC 99.262% ≥98%
Identity MS Confirmed MW match
Concentration HPLC 12.09 mg/mL 10–14 mg/mL
Endotoxin LAL <0.5 EU/mg <5 EU/mg
Sterility Membrane filtration Pass No growth

Each row represents a critical quality attribute. Purity confirms the compound is what it claims to be in terms of proportion. Identity confirms the molecular structure matches the target peptide. Concentration verifies the amount of active compound per millilitre, which is essential for accurate dosing in research protocols. Endotoxin testing (via the Limulus Amebocyte Lysate assay) screens for bacterial endotoxins that could compromise in-vivo or cell-based studies. Sterility confirms the absence of viable microorganisms.

A COA where every result falls within specification is baseline. What distinguishes a reliable supplier is who performed the testing, whether the results are independently verifiable, and whether the COA is published transparently rather than only provided on request.

Why Third-Party Testing Matters

In-house testing — where the manufacturer tests their own product — is inherently conflicted. The entity producing the compound has a financial incentive for the results to look favourable. This does not mean in-house data is always wrong, but it cannot be independently verified and should not be treated as definitive.

Third-party testing eliminates this conflict. An independent laboratory has no stake in the outcome. They test the sample as received, report what they find, and publish the results under their own name and reputation. This is the same principle behind independent financial audits: the entity being evaluated should not also be the entity certifying the evaluation.

Blind sampling strengthens this further. When the testing lab receives a sample without knowing who sent it or what the expected result should be, the potential for bias drops to near zero. This is the standard ProPeptide follows: samples are submitted to Janoshik Analytical without identifying information that could influence the analysis.

Janoshik Analytical has become the de facto standard in the peptide research community for a reason. Their methodology is consistent, their equipment is calibrated to pharmaceutical standards, and critically, every COA they issue includes a unique verification key. This is the difference between a PDF that could have been fabricated in Photoshop and a verifiable, traceable analytical record.

If a supplier cannot provide a third-party COA with a verification mechanism, treat their purity claims as unverified.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is HPLC purity?

HPLC purity is the percentage of target peptide in a sample, measured by high-performance liquid chromatography peak area. The instrument separates all components and calculates what proportion of the total is the desired compound. A higher percentage means fewer impurities and a more reliable research compound.

What purity should research peptides be?

Research-grade peptides should be at least 98% purity (≥98%) as a baseline standard. Purity of 99% or above is considered high-purity and is preferred for sensitive research applications where even trace contaminants could compromise experimental results or introduce confounding variables.

What is a certificate of analysis (COA)?

A certificate of analysis is a document issued by a testing laboratory that reports purity, identity, concentration, and other quality metrics for a specific batch of a compound. It includes the analytical methods used, the results obtained, and the specifications each result is measured against. A COA serves as third-party verification of product quality.

Who is Janoshik Analytical?

Janoshik Analytical is an independent third-party analytical laboratory widely regarded as the gold standard for peptide testing in the research community. They provide HPLC purity testing, mass spectrometry identification, and issue verifiable certificates of analysis with unique verification keys for every report.

How do I verify a COA is real?

Janoshik COAs include a unique verification key and task number that can be checked directly with the lab to confirm authenticity. This prevents suppliers from fabricating or digitally altering test results. If a COA does not include a verification mechanism, its authenticity cannot be independently confirmed.

What is the difference between HPLC and mass spectrometry?

HPLC measures how pure a sample is by quantifying the target peptide as a percentage of total content — it answers “how much is the right compound?” Mass spectrometry confirms what the molecule actually is by measuring its exact molecular weight — it answers “is this the right compound?” Together, they verify both purity and identity.
