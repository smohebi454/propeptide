GLP-1 Receptor Agonists Explained: From Single to Triple Agonists (2026)

March 20, 2026 — Dr. Lara Vestri, PharmD

Quick answer: GLP-1 receptor agonists are a class of compounds that mimic the incretin hormone GLP-1 to reduce appetite and improve glucose metabolism. The field has evolved from single agonists (semaglutide, ~15% weight loss) to dual agonists (tirzepatide, ~22%) to triple agonists (retatrutide, ~24%). Each generation adds receptor targets — and with them, new metabolic pathways and greater efficacy.

What Are GLP-1 Receptor Agonists?

GLP-1 receptor agonists belong to a class of compounds known as incretin mimetics. They replicate the activity of glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1), a hormone naturally produced by L-cells in the small intestine after food intake. Endogenous GLP-1 has a half-life of only 2–3 minutes before being degraded by the enzyme DPP-4, which limits its therapeutic usefulness in native form.

Synthetic GLP-1 agonists are engineered to resist DPP-4 degradation, extending their half-life from minutes to days or even weeks. Once bound to GLP-1 receptors in the pancreas, gut, and brain, these compounds trigger a cascade of metabolic effects: enhanced insulin secretion in response to glucose, suppressed glucagon release, delayed gastric emptying, and reduced appetite signalling in the hypothalamus.

The net result is a powerful reduction in caloric intake paired with improved glycaemic control. Originally developed for type 2 diabetes management, GLP-1 agonists have since demonstrated significant efficacy as anti-obesity agents — reshaping how researchers approach metabolic disease. The key innovation in subsequent generations has been layering additional receptor targets on top of this GLP-1 foundation, amplifying the metabolic response without requiring higher doses of any single mechanism.

Generation 1: Single GLP-1 Agonists

The first generation of GLP-1 receptor agonists includes semaglutide (marketed as Ozempic for diabetes and Wegovy for weight management) and liraglutide (marketed as Victoza and Saxenda). These compounds target only the GLP-1 receptor, relying on a single mechanistic pathway to drive results.

Semaglutide, the most studied compound in this class, demonstrated approximately 14.9% mean weight loss at the 2.4mg weekly dose in the STEP 1 trial. Liraglutide, an earlier daily-injection formulation, produced around 8% weight loss in comparable studies. Both work primarily through appetite suppression — patients eat less because the drugs slow gastric emptying and modulate hunger signalling in the brain.

The limitation is specificity. With only one receptor target, these compounds cannot address insulin resistance through alternative pathways, nor can they increase resting energy expenditure. Weight loss relies almost entirely on reducing caloric intake, which means the body loses both fat and lean tissue indiscriminately. Studies show that 20–40% of the weight lost on single GLP-1 agonists is lean mass — a significant concern for long-term metabolic health. Additionally, weight loss tends to plateau around 15% as the body adapts to the reduced caloric input. These ceiling effects motivated the development of multi-receptor approaches.

Generation 2: Dual GLP-1/GIP Agonists

Tirzepatide (marketed as Mounjaro for diabetes and Zepbound for obesity) represents the second generation: a dual agonist that activates both GLP-1 and GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide) receptors simultaneously. The addition of GIP receptor activation was a deliberate strategy to address the limitations of single-target therapy.

GIP plays a distinct role in metabolic regulation. It enhances insulin sensitivity in adipose tissue, promotes fat storage efficiency, and improves lipid metabolism in ways that GLP-1 alone cannot. When both receptors are activated together, the result is a synergistic effect — greater insulin secretion, better glucose disposal, and enhanced fat metabolism beyond what either receptor achieves independently.

The clinical data reflects this. In the SURMOUNT-1 trial, tirzepatide at the highest dose (15mg) produced an average weight loss of 22.5% over 72 weeks. Roughly 57% of participants lost 20% or more of their body weight — a threshold that was nearly unachievable with first-generation agents. The dual mechanism also appears to reduce the compensatory metabolic slowdown that limits single-agonist efficacy, allowing weight loss to continue for longer before plateauing. Generation 3: Triple GLP-1/GIP/GCGR Agonists

Retatrutide is the first triple agonist to reach advanced clinical trials, activating GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon (GCGR) receptors simultaneously. The addition of glucagon receptor agonism introduces a fundamentally different metabolic lever — one that neither single nor dual agonists can access.

Glucagon receptor activation increases resting energy expenditure by stimulating hepatic glucose production and thermogenesis. Rather than relying solely on appetite suppression to create a caloric deficit, the glucagon pathway drives the body to burn stored energy — particularly from visceral and hepatic fat deposits. This shifts the composition of weight loss: instead of losing fat and muscle indiscriminately, retatrutide produces preferential fat loss while better preserving lean tissue.

Phase 2 trial data (published in The New England Journal of Medicine, 2023) showed retatrutide at the 12mg dose produced 24.2% mean weight loss over 48 weeks. Approximately 75% of participants achieved ≥20% weight loss. Notably, a DXA substudy demonstrated a superior fat-to-lean loss ratio compared to historical data from semaglutide and tirzepatide trials. The mechanistic details of triple agonism are explored in depth in Remy Peptides' triple agonist pathway analysis. Retatrutide is currently in Phase 3 trials with results expected in 2026. What Comes Next: The Research Frontier

The evolution of GLP-1 agonists is far from over. Several next-generation approaches are in advanced clinical development, each addressing different limitations of current injectable therapies.

Oral formulations represent perhaps the most anticipated shift. Orforglipron, a small-molecule oral GLP-1 agonist developed by Eli Lilly, is currently in Phase 3 trials. Unlike peptide-based injectables, orforglipron is a non-peptide compound that can survive the digestive tract intact and be absorbed orally. Phase 2 data showed up to 14.7% weight loss — comparable to injectable semaglutide — with a once-daily pill. If Phase 3 results confirm efficacy and safety, oral GLP-1 agonists could dramatically expand patient access by eliminating injection barriers. Oral GLP-1 agonists are covered in Remy Peptides' oral obesity drug report.

Amylin combinations are another frontier. CagriSema pairs semaglutide with cagrilintide, a long-acting amylin analogue that targets a separate appetite regulation pathway in the brainstem. Phase 2 data showed approximately 17% weight loss at 32 weeks — suggesting the combination could surpass semaglutide alone. Beyond that, early-stage research is exploring quadruple agonists that add amylin receptor activity on top of the triple-agonist framework. The goal: activate four distinct metabolic pathways simultaneously to push weight loss efficacy even further while optimising body composition. These approaches remain in preclinical or early clinical stages, but they signal a clear trajectory — incretin-based therapies will continue adding complexity and specificity with each generation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does GLP-1 stand for?

GLP-1 stands for glucagon-like peptide-1. It is an incretin hormone produced by L-cells in the small intestine after food intake. GLP-1 stimulates insulin secretion, suppresses glucagon release, slows gastric emptying, and reduces appetite signalling in the brain. Synthetic GLP-1 agonists mimic this hormone with a much longer half-life.

What is the difference between single and dual GLP-1 agonists?

Single agonists like semaglutide target only the GLP-1 receptor, primarily suppressing appetite through one pathway. Dual agonists like tirzepatide add GIP receptor activation, which enhances insulin sensitivity and improves fat metabolism through a second, complementary mechanism. This results in greater weight loss (~22% vs ~15%) and better metabolic outcomes.

Is a triple agonist better than a dual agonist?

Phase 2 clinical data suggests yes. Retatrutide (triple agonist) produced approximately 24% mean weight loss versus 22% for tirzepatide (dual agonist) in comparable timeframes. The glucagon receptor component drives direct energy expenditure and preferential fat loss, offering a body composition advantage beyond total weight reduction alone.

Are GLP-1 agonists safe?

GLP-1 receptor agonists are generally well-tolerated across clinical trials. The most common side effects are gastrointestinal — nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, and constipation — which are usually transient and improve with gradual dose titration. Serious adverse events are rare. Long-term safety data exists for approved compounds like semaglutide and tirzepatide; retatrutide’s long-term safety profile is still being established in Phase 3 trials.

What is the strongest GLP-1 agonist?

By weight loss data, retatrutide (a triple GLP-1/GIP/GCGR agonist) produced the highest average weight loss at approximately 24.2% in Phase 2 trials at the 12mg dose. However, retatrutide is still investigational and has not yet received regulatory approval. Among approved compounds, tirzepatide at 15mg holds the record at approximately 22.5% mean weight loss.

Will there be a pill form of GLP-1 agonists?

Yes. Oral GLP-1 agonists are in advanced development. Orforglipron, a non-peptide oral GLP-1 agonist from Eli Lilly, is currently in Phase 3 clinical trials with results expected in 2026–2027. Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) already exists for diabetes, though at lower efficacy than injectable formulations. The next generation of oral agents aims to match or exceed injectable performance.
