What Is Retatrutide? The Triple Agonist Research Compound Explained (2026)

March 20, 2026 — Dr. Lara Vestri, PharmD

Quick Answer: Retatrutide (LY3437943) is Eli Lilly's investigational triple agonist peptide that simultaneously activates GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors. In Phase 2 trials, participants lost up to 24.2% body weight at 48 weeks — more than any other anti-obesity compound tested to date. Phase 3 TRIUMPH trials are ongoing with results expected 2025–2026.

What Is Retatrutide?

Retatrutide, designated LY3437943 by its developer Eli Lilly and Company, is an investigational peptide compound that belongs to a new class of multi-receptor agonists designed for metabolic disease research. Unlike earlier GLP-1 receptor agonists such as semaglutide, or even the dual agonist tirzepatide, retatrutide activates three distinct hormone receptors in a single molecule: the glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor, the glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) receptor, and the glucagon receptor (GCGR).

This triple-agonist design represents a fundamental shift in metabolic pharmacology. Rather than targeting appetite through a single pathway, retatrutide engages three complementary systems that regulate energy intake, insulin sensitivity, and energy expenditure. The compound first entered clinical testing in 2021 and rapidly progressed through Phase 1 and Phase 2 trials, generating results that attracted significant attention from the metabolic research community. Eli Lilly advanced the compound into a comprehensive Phase 3 program (TRIUMPH) beginning in 2024, positioning retatrutide as the most advanced triple agonist in clinical development.

The molecular architecture of retatrutide is built on a modified peptide backbone with optimised binding affinities for each of its three target receptors. This balanced activation profile is what distinguishes it from earlier experimental triple agonists that failed to achieve the correct ratio of receptor stimulation needed for clinical efficacy without unacceptable side effects.

How Retatrutide Works: Three Receptors, One Molecule

The defining feature of retatrutide is its simultaneous engagement of three metabolic receptors, each contributing a distinct physiological effect. Understanding these individual pathways clarifies why the compound produces outcomes that single- and dual-agonist therapies cannot match.

GLP-1 Receptor: Appetite and Satiety

GLP-1 receptor activation is the mechanism shared by all current approved weight-loss injectables, including semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound). When retatrutide binds the GLP-1 receptor, it slows gastric emptying, enhances insulin secretion in response to meals, and signals satiety centres in the hypothalamus. This reduces food intake by making the individual feel full earlier and for longer after eating. The GLP-1 pathway is well-characterised and provides the foundational appetite suppression component of retatrutide's mechanism.

GIP Receptor: Insulin Sensitivity and Fat Metabolism

The GIP receptor is the second target, shared with tirzepatide but absent from semaglutide. GIP receptor activation improves insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues, enhances nutrient-dependent insulin release, and appears to influence adipose tissue metabolism. Research published in Nature Metabolism suggests that GIP agonism may also modulate lipid handling in fat cells, promoting healthier fat storage patterns and reducing ectopic lipid deposition in organs like the liver. The combination of GLP-1 and GIP activation provides synergistic effects on glucose control and weight management that exceed what either receptor achieves alone.

Glucagon Receptor: Energy Expenditure

The glucagon receptor is the third and most novel component of retatrutide's mechanism — and the one that sets it apart from every approved anti-obesity therapy. Glucagon receptor activation stimulates hepatic glucose output and, critically, increases resting energy expenditure through enhanced thermogenesis and fatty acid oxidation. In practical terms, this means the body burns more calories at baseline, independent of dietary changes. For a detailed analysis of the triple agonist pathway, see this research overview by Remy Peptides. The glucagon pathway drives preferential mobilisation of stored fat for energy, which contributes to the favourable body composition data observed in clinical trials.

Clinical Trial Results

The pivotal Phase 2 data for retatrutide were published in the New England Journal of Medicine in June 2023 (Jastreboff et al., NEJM 2023;389:514-526). This 48-week, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolled 338 adults with obesity or overweight with at least one weight-related comorbidity. Participants were randomised to placebo or one of several retatrutide dose groups: 1 mg, 4 mg (with two different titration schedules), 8 mg, and 12 mg, all administered as once-weekly subcutaneous injections.

The results were striking. The 12 mg cohort achieved a mean weight loss of 24.2% from baseline at 48 weeks, with weight loss still trending downward at study end — suggesting participants had not yet reached a plateau. The 8 mg group lost 22.8%, while the 4 mg group lost 17.9%. Even the lowest 1 mg dose produced clinically meaningful reductions. In the 12 mg group, 93% of participants lost at least 10% of their body weight, and over 63% lost at least 25%.

Importantly, the trial demonstrated that slower dose titration significantly reduced the incidence and severity of these events. The most significant differentiator is timing. Retatrutide achieved 24.2% weight loss at 48 weeks, a timeframe at which semaglutide typically produces around 12–13% and tirzepatide approximately 18–20%. The rate of weight loss with retatrutide was notably steeper in the first 24 weeks, and the trajectory had not yet plateaued at study end, suggesting the final magnitude in Phase 3 trials may be even greater.

Phase 3 TRIUMPH Program

Eli Lilly launched the TRIUMPH clinical trial program in 2024, comprising four Phase 3 studies designed to evaluate retatrutide across different patient populations and clinical endpoints. These trials collectively aim to generate the data required for regulatory submissions to the FDA and EMA.

  • TRIUMPH-1: The primary obesity trial enrolling adults with BMI ≥30 (or ≥27 with comorbidities) without diabetes. This is the foundational weight-loss efficacy study with endpoints including percentage body weight change and proportion of participants achieving ≥5%, ≥10%, ≥15%, and ≥20% weight loss thresholds. Enrolment target is approximately 1,800 participants over 72 weeks of treatment.
  • TRIUMPH-2: Focused on adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes, evaluating both weight loss and glycaemic control endpoints including HbA1c reduction. This study is critical for demonstrating dual utility in the large population of patients with co-existing obesity and diabetes.
  • TRIUMPH-3: A weight maintenance study evaluating whether retatrutide can sustain weight loss over extended treatment periods and what happens to body weight after treatment discontinuation. This addresses one of the key clinical concerns with current GLP-1 therapies: rapid weight regain after stopping treatment.
  • TRIUMPH-4: Investigating retatrutide's effects on metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH, formerly NAFLD/NASH), including liver fat reduction, inflammation markers, and fibrosis endpoints. Phase 2 substudies already showed substantial liver fat reduction, making this a high-interest indication.

The TRIUMPH trial tracker maintained by Remy Peptides provides ongoing updates on enrolment status and anticipated readout dates. Top-line results from TRIUMPH-1 are expected in late 2025 or early 2026, with the remaining studies reporting through 2026. If results align with Phase 2 findings, Eli Lilly could submit regulatory filings by late 2026 or early 2027.

Why Researchers Are Watching Retatrutide

The excitement around retatrutide extends well beyond raw weight-loss numbers. Several dimensions of the compound's profile are drawing sustained interest from metabolic researchers, endocrinologists, and pharmaceutical analysts.

Body Composition

Perhaps the most meaningful advancement is the preliminary body composition data. DXA scan substudies from the Phase 2 program indicate that retatrutide produces a higher ratio of fat loss to lean mass loss compared to semaglutide and tirzepatide. This is attributed directly to the glucagon receptor component, which drives the body to preferentially oxidise stored fat for energy rather than catabolising muscle tissue. If Phase 3 DXA data confirm this pattern, it would address one of the most persistent criticisms of GLP-1 therapies: that patients lose significant amounts of metabolically valuable muscle along with fat.

MASH and Liver Fat Reduction

Phase 2 substudies showed that retatrutide reduced liver fat by over 80% in some participants, with a significant proportion achieving complete normalisation of hepatic fat content. This positions retatrutide as a potential dual-indication therapy, treating both obesity and MASH simultaneously. Given that MASH affects an estimated 5–8% of the global adult population and has limited approved treatment options, this could represent a substantial unmet medical need.

Metabolic Flexibility

The triple-receptor mechanism may confer advantages in metabolic flexibility — the body's ability to switch between fuel sources (glucose and fatty acids) depending on energy demands. Glucagon receptor activation promotes fatty acid oxidation, while GIP and GLP-1 components optimise glucose handling. This combined effect could produce metabolic improvements that persist beyond simple caloric deficit, potentially influencing cardiovascular risk markers, inflammatory profiles, and overall metabolic health. Frequently Asked Questions

What is retatrutide used for?

Retatrutide (LY3437943) is an investigational research compound being studied for the treatment of obesity and related metabolic diseases. It is not yet approved by any regulatory authority for clinical use. Current research focuses on its potential to address obesity, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH).

How much weight can you lose with retatrutide?

In Phase 2 clinical trials published in the New England Journal of Medicine, participants receiving the highest dose of retatrutide (12 mg) lost up to 24.2% of their body weight over 48 weeks. This represents the largest weight reduction observed in any anti-obesity drug trial to date. Lower doses of 4 mg and 8 mg produced 17.9% and 22.8% weight loss respectively.

Is retatrutide FDA approved?

No, retatrutide is not FDA approved as of March 2026. Eli Lilly is currently conducting Phase 3 clinical trials under the TRIUMPH program. Based on current timelines, the earliest possible regulatory submission would be 2026–2027, with potential approval in 2027 at the earliest.

How is retatrutide different from Ozempic?

Ozempic (semaglutide) is a single GLP-1 receptor agonist that works primarily through appetite suppression. Retatrutide is a triple agonist that activates GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors simultaneously. The glucagon receptor component adds a direct energy expenditure mechanism, meaning the body burns more calories at rest. This triple action produced 24.2% weight loss in trials compared to semaglutide's approximately 15%.

Who makes retatrutide?

Retatrutide is developed by Eli Lilly and Company under the research designation LY3437943. Eli Lilly is the same pharmaceutical company that developed tirzepatide (marketed as Mounjaro and Zepbound). The compound was first disclosed in Lilly's clinical pipeline in 2022 and advanced to Phase 3 trials in 2024.

When will retatrutide be available?

Retatrutide is currently in Phase 3 clinical trials under Eli Lilly's TRIUMPH program. Results from these pivotal trials are expected between 2025 and 2026. If data is positive, Eli Lilly could file for regulatory approval in 2026–2027, with the earliest commercial availability potentially in late 2027 or 2028.
